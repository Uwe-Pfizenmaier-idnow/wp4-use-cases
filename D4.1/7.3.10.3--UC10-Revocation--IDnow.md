# 4. Revocation

## 4.1 Revocation Position

UC 10 adopts a **short-lived credential design**, which eliminates the need for a formal revocation infrastructure. This design choice is justified as follows:

- **Short validity period:** All three EAAs have a validity period measured in days (typically 1–5 days, matching the hotel stay duration). The `exp` claim is set at issuance time to the expected checkout date/time.
- **Non-qualified attestations:** The EAAs are classified as EAA (not QEAA), and are therefore not subject to the revocation requirements that apply to qualified attestations under eIDAS 2.0 Art. 45e.
- **Limited reliance scope:** The EAAs are consumed by a single RP ecosystem (the hotel and its door locks). There is no scenario where a third party relies on the credential after the stay has ended.

> **ARF alignment:** This approach aligns with ARF Section 6.6, which recognises short-lived credentials as a valid alternative to revocation mechanisms when the credential's validity period is sufficiently short that the risk of reliance on a compromised credential is acceptably low.

## 4.2 Lifecycle Events

Although formal revocation is not implemented, the following lifecycle events are addressed:

### Early Checkout

If the guest checks out before the expected checkout date, the Digital Room Key EAA remains technically valid until its `exp` timestamp. However, the hotel system independently deactivates the door lock authorisation on its backend. The credential becomes inert: even if presented, the door lock controller will reject it because the room assignment has been cleared in the hotel's property management system (PMS). The EAA in the wallet becomes a non-functional artefact and will expire naturally.

### Booking Cancellation

If the booking is cancelled before check-in, the Booking Confirmation EAA remains in the wallet but is never consumed. At check-in (Phase 3), the hotel system would reject it because the `booking_id` no longer corresponds to an active reservation in the PMS. The credential expires at the original checkout date and is never verified successfully.

### Key Compromise

In the unlikely event that IDnow's signing key is compromised during a credential's validity window, IDnow would publish a key revocation notice via the Trusted Issuer List. Door readers that sync their cached key material would pick up the revocation at next sync. Given the short credential lifespan (1–5 days), the exposure window is inherently limited.
